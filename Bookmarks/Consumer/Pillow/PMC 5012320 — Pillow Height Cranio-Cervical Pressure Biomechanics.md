## Summary

## Key Takeaways
- 4 個高度測試：**H0=110mm, H1=130mm, H2=150mm, H3=170mm**
- 太高（H3）：cranial pressure 比 H0 高 30%；cervical angle 增 66.4%、lordosis distance 增 25.1%、kyphosis distance 減 43.4%（過度頭部伸展）
- 太矮（H0）：cervical pressure 比 H3 少 65%（聽落好）— 但 side sleep 時 lateral flex 加劇
- Skin contact pressure 應 < 4.20 kPa 以保 capillary perfusion
- **Sweet spot：H1–H2（130–150mm）** balance pressure 分佈

## Full Content (archived)

## Heights Tested
Four pillow heights were evaluated in the study:
- H0: 110 mm (baseline)
- H1: 130 mm
- H2: 150 mm
- H3: 170 mm

## Pressure Findings

**Cranial Region:** "The average cranial pressure at pillow height H3 was approximately 30% higher than that at H0" and showed significant differences across conditions.

**Cervical Region:** "The average cervical pressure at pillow height H0 was 65% lower than that at H3," with peak pressures at H2 and H3 significantly exceeding baseline.

## Spine Alignment Changes

As height increased from H0 to H3:
- Cervical angle increased 66.4%
- Lordosis distance increased 25.1%
- Kyphosis distance decreased 43.4%

This indicates progressive head extension and increased cervical spine curvature.

## Optimal vs. Extreme Effects

The research didn't designate a single "optimal" height. However, it noted that "skin contact pressure below 4.20 kPa was believed to allow capillary perfusion," suggesting very high pillows (H3) may cause discomfort. The data suggests intermediate heights (H1-H2) balanced pressure distribution better than extremes, though comfort optimization requires further investigation.
