## Full Content (archived)

**Quick Answer:** Side sleepers need high-loft pillows (10–14 cm) matched to shoulder width. Back sleepers need medium-loft (7–10 cm) to maintain natural neck curve. Stomach sleepers need low-loft or no pillow (0–7 cm). Pillow height and firmness together determine cervical spine alignment during sleep.

## What Is Pillow Loft?

Pillow loft refers to the height (thickness) of a pillow when lying under a person's head. It is the primary physical variable that determines whether a pillow maintains correct cervical spine alignment during sleep:

- **Low loft:** Under 7 cm (under 3 inches) — flat or thin pillows
- **Medium loft:** 7–10 cm (3–4 inches) — standard height for most back sleepers
- **High loft:** 10–14 cm (4–5.5 inches) — appropriate for most side sleepers
- **Extra-high loft:** Over 14 cm — for very broad-shouldered side sleepers or stacked pillow arrangements

Loft is not the same as how a pillow feels in its package — the relevant loft is its compressed height under actual head weight, which differs significantly between fill materials and firmness levels.

## Pillow Height by Sleep Position

### Side Sleepers: High Loft Required

When lying on your side, the head needs to be elevated to the height of the gap between the mattress and the side of the skull. This gap is determined by shoulder width — the distance from the mattress to where the neck naturally lies. A pillow that fills this gap keeps the cervical spine in the same neutral line as the rest of the spine:

- **Correct loft:** Head and neck in a neutral line — neither tilted toward the mattress (too-low pillow) nor pushed upward (too-high pillow)
- **Too-low pillow:** Head drops toward the mattress, neck in lateral flexion (ear toward shoulder) — stretches neck muscles and facet joints, causes morning neck pain
- **Too-high pillow:** Head pushed up and away from the mattress, neck in lateral flexion in the opposite direction — different muscles under stress, same outcome
- **Typical loft range for side sleepers:** 10–14 cm, with broader shoulders at the higher end

### Back Sleepers: Medium Loft

When lying on your back, the head rests on the mattress surface and needs gentle elevation to maintain the natural cervical curve (a slight forward curve) without forced flexion or extension:

- **Correct loft:** Neck maintains its natural curve — chin neither forced toward chest nor tilted back
- **Too-thick pillow:** Forces cervical spine into flexion (chin toward chest) — can cause morning neck pain, headaches, and in severe cases, airway compromise that worsens snoring
- **Too-thin pillow:** Head falls back, cervical spine in extension — causes different set of neck strains and can worsen neck and upper back tension
- **Typical loft range for back sleepers:** 7–10 cm

### Stomach Sleepers: Low Loft or No Pillow

Stomach sleeping is the most biomechanically challenging sleep position for the spine — the lumbar curve is reversed and the head is rotated 45–90° to one side for the entire night. In this position:

- A thick pillow greatly increases cervical rotation and extension — essentially unsustainable for prolonged periods without cervical strain
- A very flat pillow or no pillow at all is typically recommended for stomach sleepers — the goal is to minimize cervical elevation and therefore reduce the angle of rotation
- A pillow under the pelvis (not the head) can partially reduce lumbar strain in stomach sleeping
- **Ideal strategy:** Avoid stomach sleeping when possible (it strains both cervical and lumbar spine). Transitioning to side sleeping — even partially — is recommended for people with neck or back pain who currently sleep on their stomach

## Why Shoulder Width Matters for Side Sleepers

The single most important factor in pillow loft selection for side sleepers is shoulder width — the physical measurement from the mattress surface to the point where the neck rests when lying on the side. This is determined by shoulder breadth and, to a lesser degree, mattress firmness (how much the shoulder sinks into the mattress):

- **Narrower shoulders** (typically women and smaller-framed adults): The gap between mattress and head is smaller — lower-loft side sleeper pillows (10–11 cm) are more appropriate
- **Broader shoulders** (typically men and larger-framed adults): Greater gap requires higher-loft pillows (12–14 cm or more)
- **Practical test:** Lie on your side on your mattress with your current pillow. Have someone observe whether your head, neck, and spine form a straight line from a behind-the-head perspective. If the neck tilts downward, loft is too low. If it tilts upward, too high

## How Your Mattress Affects Pillow Choice

Mattress firmness and the degree to which it allows the shoulder to sink in directly affects the ideal pillow loft for side sleepers:

- **Firm mattress:** The shoulder sits higher above the mattress — the gap between mattress and head is larger. Requires a higher-loft pillow
- **Soft mattress:** The shoulder sinks into the mattress, reducing the gap between mattress surface and head. Requires a lower-loft pillow
- This means that changing your mattress firmness may require a corresponding change in pillow loft — a common source of confusion when people replace their mattress and subsequently experience new neck pain even with the same pillow they previously used successfully

## Pillow Fill Materials Compared

| Fill Material | Loft Stability | Feel | Temperature | Best For |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Memory foam (solid) | Excellent — doesn't compress significantly under head weight | Slow-response, contouring | Warm (especially non-gel) | Side/back sleepers needing consistent height |
| Latex (solid or shredded) | Excellent (solid); adjustable (shredded) | Responsive, springy | Cooler than memory foam | Side sleepers wanting responsiveness |
| Down (goose/duck) | Poor — compresses significantly; needs regular fluffing | Soft, moldable | Good insulation; can feel warm | Stomach sleepers; back sleepers who prefer very soft feel |
| Down alternative (polyester) | Fair — compresses more than foam, less than down | Soft, adaptable | Good breathability | Allergy-sensitive sleepers; budget option |
| Buckwheat | Good — adjustable by adding/removing fill | Firm, conforms to shape | Excellent breathability | Side/back sleepers wanting adjustability; hot sleepers |
| Shredded memory foam | Good — adjustable, maintains more loft than down | Softer than solid foam | Warmer than down alternatives | Side/back sleepers wanting customizable loft |

## Pillow Firmness

Pillow firmness (how much resistance the pillow provides under head weight) works together with loft:

- **Firm pillows** resist compression — they maintain their loft under head weight. Better for side sleepers who need consistent height support throughout the night
- **Soft pillows** compress under head weight — their actual functional loft is lower than their nominal height. Better for stomach sleepers or back sleepers who want cushioning without height
- A pillow that starts at the right loft but compresses to insufficient height during the night will produce the same neck strain as a permanently too-low pillow
- **For side sleepers:** Prioritize pillows that maintain their loft under sustained compression — solid memory foam, latex, or firm down alternatives perform best

## Combination Sleepers

[Combination sleeper](https://mattressmiracle.ca/blogs/mattress-miracle-blog/best-pillow-back-and-side-sleepers) s (those who shift positions during the night) face a genuine challenge: the ideal pillow loft for side sleeping is higher than for back sleeping. Options:

- **Adjustable-fill pillows:** Shredded memory foam, buckwheat, and some latex pillows can be adjusted by adding or removing fill — allowing a compromise height or two separate configurations
- **Contour pillows with dual zones:** Some pillows are shaped with a higher loft section for side sleeping and a recessed center for back sleeping
- **Compromise approach:** Use a medium-high loft pillow — slightly insufficient for optimal side sleeping, slightly too high for back sleeping — minimizing the worst-case alignment in both positions
- **Position prioritization:** Most combination sleepers have a dominant position where they fall asleep and spend the most time. Optimizing for that dominant position first is the practical starting point

## When to Replace Your Pillow

- Down and polyester pillows: Every 1–2 years. These materials compress and lose their loft significantly faster than foam or latex
- Memory foam (solid): Every 3–4 years. Foam loses its resilience over time — when a solid foam pillow has visible permanent deformation, replace it
- Latex (solid): 4–6+ years. Latex is the most durable pillow material
- **Simple replacement test:** Fold the pillow in half. If it doesn't spring back immediately, it has lost sufficient resilience to provide consistent loft support — replace it
- New neck pain or stiffness on waking that wasn't present previously is often a signal that a pillow has deteriorated rather than that a new sleep problem has emerged

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: I sleep on my side but sometimes on my back — what pillow height should I use?**

For combination sleepers who primarily side sleep, optimize for side sleeping loft (10–14 cm based on shoulder width) and accept that it will be slightly too high for back sleeping periods. The alternative — optimizing for back sleeping at 7–10 cm — will be meaningfully insufficient for side sleeping and is likely to produce neck strain. If the height difference causes significant discomfort in your secondary position, consider an adjustable-fill pillow (shredded memory foam or buckwheat) that lets you fine-tune loft, or a contour pillow with a recessed center for back sleeping and raised edges for side sleeping.

**Q: Are cervical/contour pillows better than regular pillows?**

Cervical contour pillows (with a recessed center and raised edges, or a distinct neck roll) can be excellent for back sleepers because they provide targeted support for the cervical curve rather than uniform elevation of the head. Research on cervical pillows for neck pain is mixed — they help some people significantly and do nothing for others. The key is whether the contour height matches your cervical measurements. For side sleepers, most standard contour pillows are designed for back sleeping and may not provide sufficient loft. There are also cervical pillows designed specifically for side sleeping — these can be beneficial if the dimensions match your shoulder-to-head gap. As with regular pillows, the right contour pillow is highly individual.

**Q: How much should I spend on a pillow?**

Pillow price ranges enormously — from under $20 for basic polyester fill to $200+ for premium latex or specialty cervical designs. For most people, the sweet spot is $50–$120 for a quality fill material (solid or shredded memory foam, latex, or premium down alternative) that will maintain loft for 2–4 years. The highest-impact pillow improvement for most people is not price — it's choosing the correct loft and firmness for their sleep position. A $50 latex pillow at the right height will outperform a $150 memory foam pillow at the wrong height for neck pain prevention. In Canada, quality pillows in the $60–$100 range from brands like Casper, Endy, and various Canadian retailers offer good value without the premium markup of specialty sleep brands.
